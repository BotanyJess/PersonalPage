Floristic Inventory

Botanical collections provide foundational data on diversity and distribution of plants. The resulting herbarium specimens provide examples of phenotypic variation, phenology and material from which DNA can be extracted. Comprehensive surveys provide a depth of data that episodic local surveys and citizen science do not replace. As part of my master’s degree, I performed a botanical inventory of a 27,330 square km area in east-central Idaho, from 2010-2012. With assistance from my graduate advisor, Dr. Ron Hartman, we collected and pressed 11,235 specimens, from which I identified 1,349 vascular plant taxa.

Polemonium elusum J. J. Irwin & R. L. Hartman

The day that I collected a specimen of an undescribed Polemonium, I was not aware of its distinction. It was not until the following winter, while keying plants, that I realized I had pressed something special. During the previous fall semester, I was angry at my advisor’s requirement for ‘botanical Latin’. The International Code of Botanical Nomenclature had recently lifted the requirement that publications containing newly described species include a paragraph in Latin. Most notably, I thought to myself, “I’m never finding an undescribed species anyway, so why must I take this class?!”

Polemonium elusum is a peculiar species in that it has a small geographic distribution of approximately 260 sq km, but grows in a broad range of habitat types and elevations. Is Polemonium elusum rapidly contracting or expanding its range?
